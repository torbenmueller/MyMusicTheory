Period Form

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart - Piano Sonata No. 11 in A major, KV 331 (300i)

Start with a 2 bar basic idea, usually pretty harmonically stable (stay on the tonic). The basic idea is your main motif, it is the main identifying piece of your theme.

You respond to it with a 2 bar contrasting idea. This is usually new material or variation on the basic idea. It feels like we're going somewhere, we're moving forward. It feels like a question. By the end of the phrase we should feel like we're unresolved. This whole first phrase (bar 1 - 4) is called the antecedent. It gets responded to and answered by the consequent (bar 5 - 8).

At the beginning of the consequent (bar 5 and 6) you return to your basic idea. You can vary it, you can change it, but really at least the beginning of the basic idea should be the same.

And then finally in bar 7 and 8 we have the cadence. It could be a perfect or a half cadence, but it should feel like a pretty strong sense of punctuation. It is a good idea for your cadence to be the opposite of whatever happened in your contrasting idea (e.g. if your contrasting idea ended on the V chord, you probably want to end your whole theme on the I chord).
